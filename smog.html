Understanding Smog & Soot

An overview of two major types of air pollution

Smog

Smog is a type of air pollution that results from a mixture of pollutants, including ground-level ozone, fine particulate matter, nitrogen oxides, volatile organic compounds, and sulfur dioxide. There are two main types of smog:

  • Photochemical Smog: Forms when pollutants from vehicle emissions, industrial activities, and other sources react with sunlight.
  • Sulfurous Smog: Also known as "London smog" or "industrial smog," it occurs in areas with a high concentration of industrial activity, where coal or other fossil fuels are burned.

Smog can have serious health and environmental impacts, including respiratory problems, cardiovascular effects, environmental damage, and reduced visibility.

Soot

Soot, also known as particulate matter or black carbon, is a type of air pollution consisting of tiny particles of carbonaceous material emitted from combustion processes. Sources of soot include vehicle engines, industrial facilities, wood-burning stoves and fireplaces, and agricultural burning.

Soot can have adverse effects on health, contributing to respiratory and cardiovascular problems. It also plays a role in climate change by absorbing sunlight and contributing to localized warming.
